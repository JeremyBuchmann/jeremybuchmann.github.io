Hack4Reno Recap

Nov 09, 2015
by Jeremy Buchmann
in misc

The Hack4Reno Summit was devised as a data and project sharing event to be used as a thought-builder for the annual Hack4Reno Hackathon in the spring. Hack4Reno happened last Saturday; here's my recap:

Ashley Hand (LA Civic Innovator and Architect) gave the keynote and pointed to some interesting facts:

  • Most people (72%) trust local government (as opposed to federal).
  • Government communication is often the problem, not the services themselves. Example: surveyed opinion of snowplow service in some city (Cleveland?) went up after the city implemented a real-time snowplow location and plowing schedule online, even though the plowing service didn’t change.
  • Government doesn’t always communicate within itself. Example: a group of state employees were given the task to find a data set from another department that none of them worked in within 10 minutes. They tried Googling, calling co-workers, and other methods, but none of them got the data in time. The presenter got the data in 30 seconds from a data portal that none of the participants knew existed.
  • There are benefits to testing “smarter city” ideas in a “living lab” environment before rolling them out city-wide.

Andrew Clinger (Reno City Manager) showed off the new city data portal which runs on Socrata. It’s nice, but terribly expensive (almost \$100k…not sure if that’s once or per year).

Will Kurt (Data Scientist) gave an eye-opening talk on mathematical Expectation vs. Variance as it relates to evaluating risk and making predictions based on data.

Anjeanette Damon (reporter for RGJ) discussed data-driven and data-enabled journalism. Example: using real-estate data to discover short-sale scams perpetrated by unscrupulous real estate agents.

Joe Chavez (Software Engineer) talked about the nuts and bolts of hackathons, both public and intra-organization, and how to participate in one.

Jessica Carsten from Socrata gave a great talk on data portals. Main points:

  • Data is the tool, not the end result.
  • Internal users may/should be the primary end users.
  • Start with what you’ve got.
  • Don’t make public data 2nd class; eat your own dog food.
  • Automate publishing, otherwise you are just contributing to future bit rot.
  • Push data to existing portals where people already go, i.e. real estate data to Zillow.

Keith Anderson (President of Trinity Applied Internet) showed how to create APIs from static data (like a table on a web page) with Kimono which was really, really awesome.

Kim Robinson discussed regional planning.

Ashley Hennefer (Writer & Researcher) talked about how to participate in hackathons without being a programmer. Designers, testers, writers, presenters and various subject-matter experts as also needed.

Lynne Barker (Sustainability Manager at City of Reno) discussed tools such as mysidewalk for evaluating sustainability needs and pointed out that manual data gathering is expensive and should be automated where possible.

Alicia Barber (Historian) discussed the presentation of historical data using mobile apps which allow self-guided tours with photos, oral histories, and text.

Matthew Bertman from Filament discussed his company’s wireless IoT products which are aiming for as much as 10 miles of line-of-sight range with 3-5 years of battery life.

Kelsey Fitzgerald (Science Writer) discussed the challenges and benefits of crowdsourcing data, specifically, a species survey of the Truckee River. The gist was that crowdsourcing is best for creating a conversation around data, and may be the only way to obtain data if you have no money. In terms of obtaining the data, it is slow, difficult to keep people motivated, and can lead to erroneous data.

Colin Robertson (Curator) discussed the intersections of art and data and data-inspired artists.

Project Baseline discussed their efforts to use SCUBA divers as citizen scientists for gathering data on change in aquatic environments.

Some students from UNR presented GIS data from the RenoTracks project.
