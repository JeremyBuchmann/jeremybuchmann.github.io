Beer and The Beauty of Tables

May 07, 2017
by Jeremy Buchmann
in beer

My grandfather was a machinist. He started out in the 1930s working on airplane engines and later worked as a tool and die maker, moldmaker, and millwright into the 1980's. Even in retirement, he took odd jobs fixing tractors and rebuilding various machine parts. Eventually, the time came for him to get rid of a lot of his old tools, and he gave me his old tool chest that he had purchased and been using since the 30s. In the middle of this chest is an odd-looking vertical drawer that would look out of place in any modern tool chest.

(this is not my chest, but it's very similar)

This strange vertical drawer was designed (I believe) to hold a book called simply "Machinery's Handbook".

First published in 1914, Machinery's Handbook is a compendium of information for machinists such as trigonometry, thread sizes, cutting speeds, feed speeds, metal alloys, and more. Even a casual perusal of this book will show you that much of this information is delivered in tables.

The beauty of printed tables is their simplicity of use; find the column, find the row, find the value. You don't need to train someone how to use them, they don't need batteries, and you can copy them, tape them on things, and make notes in the margins.

The analog nature of "in-between" values makes tables seem out-of-place in a world of screens with 20 digits of resolution, but this provides a cogent reminder that most of those digits are not important. Billions of smooth-running, high-performance machines were built from data on printed tables with four digits of resolution.

So how does all this relate to beer? In brewing, there are a lot of formulas for calculations such as mash pH, water chemistry, original gravity, IBUs, and hydrometer correction. Many of these formulas are complex and better handled by software such as Beersmith or online calculators, but for some cases, it's hard to beat the simplicity of printed tables.

For example, a hydrometer correction is a simple two-variable lookup. If you know the temperature of the wort sample and it's hydrometer reading, you can lookup the corrected value in a table.
