Beer and The Beauty of Tables

May 07, 2017
by Jeremy Buchmann
in beer

My grandfather was a machinist. He started out in the 1930’s working on airplane engines and worked as a tool and die maker, moldmaker, and millwright into the 1980’s. Even in retirement, he took odd jobs fixing tractors and rebuilding various machine parts. Eventually, the time came for him to get rid of a lot of his old tools. He gave me his old tool chest that he had purchased and been using since the 1930’s. In the middle of this chest is an odd-looking vertical drawer that would look out of place in any modern tool chest. This strange vertical drawer was designed (I believe) to hold a book called simply “Machinery’s Handbook”. First published in 1914, Machinery’s Handbook is a compendium of information for machinists such as trigonometry, thread sizes, cutting speeds, feed speeds, metal alloys, and more. Even a casual perusal of this book will show you that much of this information is delivered in tables.

The beauty of printed tables is their simplicity of use; find the column, find the row, find the value. You don’t need to train someone how to use them, they don’t need batteries, and you can copy them, tape them on things, and make notes in the margins.

The analog nature of “in-between” values makes tables seem out-of-place in a world of screens with

Every bit that passes through a computer is fundamentally an analog signal. It is only through our interpretation that the signal represents a 1 or 0

In brewing, there are a lot of formulas (mash pH, water chemistry, original gravity, IBUs, hydrometer). Much of this can be handled by software such as Beersmith or online calculators, but it’s hard to beat the simplicity of printed tables.
